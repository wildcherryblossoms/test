DISAPPEARING GWANGJU


DRAWING/RESEARCH/PHOTOGRAPHY, 2010
Gaia Gallery, Gwangju, Korea

Found in the gaps between high-rise apartment towers in Gwangju, South Korea are thriving colonies of low-slung houses. Dating from the 1960s, these areas of affordable housing had developed alongside the industrializing city. Under President Park Chung Hee's New Village Movement (Saemaul Undong), traditional rural homes ― the chogajib, a thatched roof hanok ― were ubiquitously standardized using widely available industrial materials like corrugated metal and concrete block. Today, these settlements house the urban poor, predominantly the elderly. Generally viewed as bland, outdated structures, and often as a painful reminder of a period of political tyranny, these tenements - known as slabijibs - have nevertheless acquired their own character through decades of inhabitation. Modern renovations and basic repairs have altered the standard building envelope, and even as a slabijib is abandoned, it is transformed by the beautiful texture of decay. There are many reasons why people still live in these vibrant communities.

Disappearing Gwangju is a documentation exploring the conditions and fate of Saemaul dwelling through orthographic drawings, photographs, and compiled stories.

Disappearing Gwangju was exhibited in the GAIA Art Gallery in Gwangju, South Korea.
